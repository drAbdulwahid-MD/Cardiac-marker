Cardiac Markers - Summery Note/CVS-PC-I

1. What Are Cardiac Markers?

Cardiac markers are biochemical substances released into the blood when myocardial cells are damaged. They help in diagnosing, assessing severity, and monitoring cardiac diseases, especially myocardial infarction (MI).

2. Important Cardiac Markers and Their Characteristics

A. Troponins (cTnI & cTnT) – Gold Standard for MI

Key Point: Troponins are the most specific and sensitive markers for myocardial infarction.
Feature Detail
Types cTnI (cardiac troponin I), cTnT (cardiac troponin T)
Rise in Blood 3–6 hours after MI
Peak Levels 12–24 hours
Duration 7–14 days (important for late MI diagnosis)

B. CK-MB – Used for Reinfarction Detection

Feature Detail
Specificity Less than troponins (also found in skeletal muscle)
Duration 2–3 days
Clinical Use Detecting reinfarction (due to short half-life)

C. Myoglobin – Fastest but Nonspecific

Feature Detail
Rise in Blood 1–2 hours (fastest marker)
Duration 24 hours
Clinical Use Early MI detection but not specific

3. Cardiac Marker Timeline: When to Order Each Test?

Marker Onset (hrs) Peak (hrs) Duration (days) Clinical Use
Myoglobin 1–2 6–9 1 Early MI detection
Troponins 3–6 12–24 7–14 Gold standard for MI
CK-MB 3–6 12–24 2–3 Reinfarction detection

4. Mnemonics to Remember Cardiac Markers

"Troponins Take Time, CK-MB Comes Back, Myoglobin Moves Fast"

Explanation:

  • Troponins → Appear late but stay longest.
  • CK-MB → Used for reinfarction.
  • Myoglobin → Fastest rise but nonspecific.

5. Final Exam Tip

  • Troponins are the most important marker for MI.
  • CK-MB is best for reinfarction detection.
  • BNP is used for heart failure, NOT MI!
